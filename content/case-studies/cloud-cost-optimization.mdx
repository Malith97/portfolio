## The Need for Cloud Cost Optimization
As cloud adoption grows, costs grow with it.

Without strong controls, cloud spend can drift faster than teams realize, affecting budgets, delivery flexibility, and year-end financial targets.

Effective cloud cost optimization is about maximizing value while keeping reliability and scalability intact.

![Cloud cost visibility and governance baseline](/case-studies/cloud-cost-optimization/img1.webp)

## Rising Cloud Costs and Lack of Visibility
As cloud environments expanded in my organization, so did monthly spend.

Teams were moving quickly, launching new services and provisioning resources continuously, but cost visibility and centralized controls were not keeping pace.

In practice, this created a pattern of avoidable waste:

- Individual and distributed test environments were provisioned freely.
- Non-production environments were left running overnight and on weekends.
- Higher-spec machines were requested for workloads that did not need them.
- Environments were not consistently destroyed after use.
- Kubernetes clusters had over-provisioned pods/nodes, orphaned volumes, and scaling inefficiencies.

These issues were amplified by fragmented ownership and missing governance standards, making forecasting and cost control difficult.

## Data-Driven, Collaborative Solutions
To address both rising costs and visibility gaps, I used a structured approach centered on data, cross-team collaboration, automation, and continuous improvement.

Team context and baseline:
- Cross-functional team of around 60 people (engineering, QA, product, business, DevOps).
- Monthly spend baseline around $20K with projected growth to ~$24K if unchanged.
- Initial reduction target: at least 30% in monthly spend.

### Data Collection and Analysis
I started by auditing billing and utilization data deeply.

Using AWS Cost Explorer, custom tagging standards, and monitoring dashboards (Prometheus/Grafana), we mapped cost drivers by resource group and environment.

This surfaced recurring inefficiency categories:
- Idle databases
- Oversized and unused snapshots
- Outdated images
- Off-hour runtime waste
- Network transfer inefficiencies
- Stale release/storage data
- Over-provisioned instances
- Excess Kubernetes node counts
- Orphaned persistent volumes
- Missed opportunities for Spot usage

![Rightsizing and scaling optimization actions](/case-studies/cloud-cost-optimization/img2.webp)

### Fixing Inefficiencies Through Automation and Governance
After identifying hotspots, we implemented targeted improvements with policy enforcement and automation.

#### 1. Idle Databases
Lambda automation monitored CloudWatch CPU and connection metrics.

If a development database stayed idle for over 4 continuous hours, it was automatically stopped to reduce compute spend.

#### 2. Oversized and Unused Snapshots
We applied age-based tagging (`usage=no_use_30`, `usage=no_use_60`) and automated deletion after defined retention thresholds.

Snapshots tagged unused for 30 days were marked first, escalated at 60 days, and deleted after extended inactivity windows (for example 75 days) with pre-delete reporting.

Deletion actions included review reporting to avoid accidental data loss, and snapshot size reviews reduced redundant retained data.

#### 3. Outdated Images
ECR lifecycle policies and image scanning workflows removed deprecated images.

Outdated AMIs were also cleaned regularly, with security baseline alignment and patch hygiene maintained through scheduled automation.

#### 4. Resource Uptime and Off-Hour Usage
CloudWatch schedules and Lambda automation shut down non-critical resources outside business hours.

EC2 and EKS non-production resources were scaled down during nights and weekends by schedule.

Overrides were managed centrally for workloads that required extended uptime.

#### 5. High Network Costs
We reduced unnecessary transfer spend by regional placement improvements and tighter cross-region replication controls.

Critical resiliency traffic remained intact; non-essential transfer was minimized.

#### 6. Data Retention and Release Hygiene
S3 lifecycle policies automated transitions and cleanup based on access patterns and retention rules.

Unneeded logs and stale release data were removed, and colder data moved to lower-cost storage tiers.

#### 7. Over-Provisioned Instances
Provisioning guidance was tightened so requested specs matched actual workload needs.

For example, request patterns like 6 vCPUs for work that needed only 4 were corrected through guidance and reviews.

We standardized lower-cost instance classes where appropriate for dev/test workloads.

#### 8. Excessive Kubernetes Node Counts
Cluster Autoscaler behavior was tuned with improved thresholds and scale-down rules.

Pod requests/limits were standardized to improve bin packing and reduce over-allocation.

#### 9. Orphaned Persistent Volumes
Scheduled checks identified unattached EBS volumes for review and safe cleanup.

This removed long-running storage waste while preserving data safety checks.

#### 10. Spot Instances
Spot capacity was introduced for build workloads (especially short-lived CI jobs), with fallback logic to on-demand capacity on interruption.

This was especially effective for build jobs in the 15-minute to 1-hour range.

Spot fleets and instance diversification improved both cost savings and reliability.

## Ensuring Sustainable Savings
Cost reduction is not durable without operating discipline.

We kept savings stable with real-time dashboards, alerting, policy-driven guardrails, and recurring cross-functional cost reviews.

Key sustainability practices:
- Continuous monitoring with anomaly alerting
- Automated governance for tagging and provisioning
- Regular stakeholder reviews and accountability loops
- Ongoing cost-awareness training
- Continuous automation expansion for lifecycle tasks
- Strategy updates aligned to evolving product and usage patterns

![Sustained optimization outcomes and operating model](/case-studies/cloud-cost-optimization/img3.webp)

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
This effort demonstrated that meaningful savings can be achieved without sacrificing platform reliability.

In this portfolio case-study framing, the initiative delivered a **35%** spend reduction, equivalent to roughly **$75K/year** in annualized savings.

In the original operating window discussed in the source article, monthly spend improvements were also tracked from roughly $20K to $12K during active optimization cycles.

Key lesson: cost optimization is not a one-time project. It is an engineering capability that must be maintained through shared ownership, visibility, governance, and automation.

## Key Takeaways
- Cloud cost optimization is a shared responsibility across engineering and leadership.
- Visibility and transparent cost allocation are the foundation for effective decisions.
- Governance must be ongoing to prevent cost regression.
- Automation is what makes savings repeatable and sustainable.
